Introduction

Prologue: putting the «administrative detention» in a spatial and chronological frame

Notions like "workshy" attitudes, "alcohol abuse" and "idleness" became the trigger categories evoking and justifying administrative detention in most Swiss cantonal corpus juris. It was an instrument giving the authorities power to confine individuals amongst the lower social strata in institutions of different kinds, in order to discipline and correct them. Often, no cantonal court was involved, so it disregarded the constitutional principle of separation of powers: But the alleged "morally unstable" had to be excluded from the society, re-educated in a closed environment and afterwards integrated in the public once more. In order to fulfill this "societal task", the communities and the cantons developped different strategies to detain these persons. Given that the founding and upkeep of institutions such as cantonal prisons, workhouses and psychiatric facilities were highly costly, not every canton was willing or able to invest on their own grounds. As a result, the authorities sent detainees to adjacent cantons and helped the corresponding institution's board to operate at full capacity and keep costs at a manageable level. This was one of the key-elements to establish what is called mulitfunctional institutions, where sentenced criminals and administrative detainees were mixed together.

Img. 1.1 – Archives de l'Etat de Fribourg (AEF)/Staatsarchiv Freiburg (StAF), EB Div Photos 10 I, Bellechasse, Photographies aériennes de Bellechasse, 1948, Section 1, image 3 : « vue générale ». [Photographe : Swissair Lufbild, Verlag O. Wyrsch, Wabern b. Bern]

Amongst many tasks, the Independent Expert Commission is meant to gather quantitative data regarding the authorities and institutions which were responsible for confining administrative detainees. By relying on already existing indexes for the targeted years 1933, the 1940ies, 1954, 1965 and 1980, the topography of institutions - with their locations and basic claims such as name, purpose, capacity and responsible public authorities - could be reconstructed. Working with historical data meant of course, that the indexes vary between years and document each authors perspective. The gathered data has been transferred with the help of DensityDesign into interactive maps and visualizations, which will lead to a more profound understanding on the spatial and chronological context of the administrative detention.

The narrative develops along four chapters: It starts with setting the indexes into a timeline and explaining the different perspectives of the sources. Next, the data is brought onto maps and in combination with various aspects like types of institution, capacities or distribution by gender. By choosing various correlations in the following segment, the different kinds of institutions can be characterized thoroughly. The final segment explains, by referring to the canton of Fribourg with its facilities of Bellechasse, how authorities detained individuals in institutions and reveals the network amongst cantons. Each visualization offers various sample years and categories to explore. There is even more in-depth information at hand by clicking on elements within the visualizations, which will lead to the glossary-section. Furthermore, the datasets as well as each visualization's documentation are available for download.

Timeline

The timeline covers the period from 1930 to 1980. Our intent is to provide an overview of developments in the social history of Switzerland during that period. The timeline also charts the relevant legislative developments and, at the same time, indicates which detention facilities were opened or closed during those years. The simultaneous display of events at these three levels helps to situate the phenomenon of administrative detention in a larger historical scale.

The chart represent majour events happened between 1930 and 1990. Move your mouse cursor over any element to learn name and date of events, click on it to reveal further information in the side panel.

Level 1, Social history: The introduction of social security measures in the 1940s (Old Age and Survivor Benefits Insurance, Disability Insurance, Family Allowances, Unemployment Insurance) - measures still in effect today - and Switzerland's ratification of the European Convention on Human Rights in 1974, had an important influence on the decisions taken by the authorities, as they offered viable alternatives for dealing with poverty and so called social deviance.

Level 2, Legislation: This level shows the federal and cantonal laws that were of relevance to administrative detention measures. It makes clear the extent to which the use of administrative detention was influenced by cantonal structures. At the same time, a study of the wording of the laws reveals that the term "administrative detention" was only rarely employed; very often character descriptions are used for purposes of categorisation (e.g. indolent, dissolute, drunkard). This made it possible to take such social "abnormalities" and morally tinged categorisations as a basis for ordering administrative detention.

Level 3, Detention facilities: The majority of the detention facilities were established in the 19th and early 20th centuries. Between 1930 and 1980, they were supplemented by smaller facilities, which were used mainly for psychiatric cases. Beginning in the 1970s, there is a discernible trend towards the closing of district prisons, juvenile correction facilities, and labour colonies (presumably for purposes of centralisation under intercantonal conventions on correctional measures).

Sources

This segment is about the sources, their quality and validity. The information on which the charts are based was gathered from five sources respectively lists of the facilities and institutions that were used for correctional purposes and the execution of administrative detention orders. Those directories were compiled at different times and by different authors. Common to all of the directories is that they cover the whole of Switzerland. In addition, at the time when they were compiled, they all served as guides (reference works) for committing agencies and are comparable in the amount of detail they include.

The chart represent the surveyed facilities and their corresponding status year by year. scroll down to switch from this mosaic plot to a sankey diagram, click on any block or flow to reveal the list of facilities beloinging to the corresponding group in the side panel.

They vary widely in the perspectives from which they were prepared though, and thus focus on different types of institutions. Three of the directories contain all of the data we needed for preparing the charts (1954, 1965, 1980). For the other two, the lists of 1933 and the 1940ies, it was necessary for us to consult additional sources as well, in order to gather the complete information. Poorhouses are mentioned only in the first source, whereas the list of 1980 sheds light only on prisons. This also illustrates one of the weaknesses of these sources, since e.g. poor houses played a decisive role in dealing with poverty among the elderly and for the internment of persons of advanced age.

In addition to the already mentioned mosaic-diagramm, the following sankey-diagramm shows the shifts of the observed institutions between the surveyed years. A total of 648 institutions are enlisted in the five directories, although not all of them necessarily appear in each individual survey (directory). How many of the institutions were open, but not surveyed by the authors and therefore are a blind spot in the data? The 1933 source is one of the most representative, as we find there all categories of facilities (poorhouses, juvenile reformatory, labour colonies, correctional labour facilities, institutions for persons with special needs, psychiatric facilities, alcoholic asylums) that are of interest to us in connection with the use of administrative detention. The category of "poorhouses" was left out in the other sources (and therefore labelled as uncertain); the focus of those sources is more on correctional labour and correctional facilities. In addition, some of the institutions were closed over the years, while new ones were opened.

Facility purposes

The institutions were often used to confine multiple categories of individuals ("drunkards", "indolent", "dissolute") who were interned on various grounds (under criminal law, civil law, administrative detention/cantonal internment laws). The Bellechasse facilities are typical of such institutions. The Bellechasse complex included a prison, an alcohol rehabilitation facility, and a correctional labour facility.

Distribution by facility type

The following types of detention facilities existed: forced labour institutions, labour colonies, juvenile reformatories, prisons, psychiatric institutions, municipal poorhouses (mentioned only in the 1933 source) and facilities for persons with special needs. All of these facility types were used for the confinement of administrative detainees. Conditions varied widely from canton to canton. With a closer look on the year 1965, prisons were quite evenly spread all over Switzerland, because most districts had their own small prison. Furthermore, educational institutions were located in urban areas. Many forced labour institutions happened to be in previously drained rural and plain areas such as the Seeland (Etablissements de Bellechasse, Anstalten Witzwil, St. Johannsen, Arbeiterheim Tannenhof) or the Orbebene (Etablissements de la Plaine de l'Orbe).

Institutional capacities

With the help of this map we would like to show how many places were available in each of the facilities. Here also, one can see how broad a range of facilities there was, from the smallest with a capacity of up to 19 places to the larger-sized ones with capacities of 150 or even 200 and more inmates.

The map shows facilities and their respective capacity. hover or click on any element to reveal further information. hover on legend items to highlight corresponding elements.

In 1954, considering forced labor institutions, labor colonies and juvenile reformatories only, almost one fifth of the available space for inmates in Switzerland was situated in just five facilities of the Seeland area: Etablissements de Bellechasse, Institutions of Witzwil, Arbeiterheim Tannenhof, St. Johannsen and Staatliche Erziehungsheim für Knaben. That is 1520 places of an estimated total of 9000. Many inmates of these institutions were meant to work on the plains and contribute to the income of the institution. Today, Witzwil and Bellechasse are two of the largest producers of agricultural goods in Switzerland.

Distribution by denomination

Religious confession and gender were two of the main criteria for deciding on the placement of detainees within the facilities: we distinguish between interdenominational, Protestant and Catholic ones. In the case of the poor houses the denomination remained often unknown. Interdenominational institutions were most likely state run and rather of large size, whereas Catholic and Protestant affiliated facilities were rather privately funded.

The map shows facilities and their respective religious denomination. hover or click on any element to reveal more details.

For example, in 1965 a total of 84 percent of the institutions were interdenominational (the lion's share being district prisons), 11 percent Catholic and 5 percent Protestant. Facilities that were restricted to a single confession were in most cases educational ones. Amongst the Catholic ones are Schweizerisches Mädchenheim in Richterswil ZH, Kinderdörfli Rathausen LU and Institution Bon Pasteuer in Lully FR and amongst the Protestant ones are the Mütter- und Kinderheim in Lausen BL, Landheim Brüttisellen in Bassersdorf ZH and Gemeinnütziges Erziehungsheim Schloss Kasteln in Oberflachs AG.

Distribution by gender

Gender was the second major criterion that was considered when choosing a facility. Sticking to the targeted year of 1965, the overall picture indicates, that 59 percent were for both genders, 27 for male and 14 for female inmates only. A considerable amount of facilities with places for both men and women were district prisons. By leaving this type aside, the proportion would shift distinctively towards one half of the total for male inmates, and a quarter for female respectively for both gender each.

Correlations

This graphic representation of the data shows how frequent the combinatin of two features occur. The bigger the dot, the more institutions of the sample share a certain combination. The above mentioned fact that district prisons are often used for both men and women can be observed by the size of the dot when comparing "gender" and "type". In contrary, prisons for women only are a relatively seldom phenomenon. This possibility of freely combining the data collected allows for a more in-depth understanding of the proportions and characteristics of the detention facilities.

By leaving this chapter the image on the side gives an impression of the decision-makers involved in the use of administrative detention, that is, of the bodies of authority which decided whether an administrative detention order would be issued. In addition, it shows the wide range of authorities that were involved: cantonal authorities, the police, guardianship offices, welfare authorities for the poor, etc. The bodies responsible for the internment of detainees differed from canton to canton, and from case to case. It also shows how diffuse the rules on the use of administrative detention in Switzerland actually were.

Internment and release

Placement in an institution

In Switzerland, the administrative detention procedures were based on a heterogeneous and complex set of cantonal statutes and on the Swiss Civil Code. Each Canton developed its own procedures and established the authorities involved at local, regional and cantonal level. Thus, the process leading to administrative detention took up many different forms.

Img. 4.1 – Archives de l'Etat de Fribourg (AEF)/Staatsarchiv Freiburg (StAF), EB Div Photos 13, Bellechasse, Ancienne colonie, maison de rééducation au travail, 20-21 janvier 1938, Section V, image 1 : «Dalle des combles».

For example in the Canton of Fribourg, as shown in the articles 30 to 43 of the Loi sur l’assistance from July 17th 1951, the various detention measures were decided mainly by the prefect, who represented the government in his district and was responsible for maintaining public order. This kind of procedure was subject to few formal requirements and was often carried out in an expeditious way. It set forth that the prefect had to conduct the investigation, mainly drawing on the allegations of the police and the public authorities. In the end, he alone decided on the detention. The person reported was granted a minimum of legal guarantees consisting of the right to be heard and the right to appeal to the government. The citizens of the Canton of Fribourg were usually detained in Bellechasse, a correctional facility that also took charge of detainees from other Swiss Cantons. In other words, the cantonal governments concluded agreements with one another for the execution of administrative detention orders outside their own territory.

Intercantonal connections

This chart shows which cantons worked in cooperation with each other for dealing with administrative detention matters (e.g. detainees sending from different cantons into the canton of Fribourg). In some cases it was not possible to determine from the sources in which facility a detainee was ultimately interned. These cases have been marked as "unknown". Another interesting feature of this chart is that it shows the existence of intercantonal connections that differed from those later agreed upon in the Intercantonal Convention on Criminal Correction. This cooperation between the cantons provided a solution for the execution of measures such as administrative detention even where there were no detention facilities in operation on the territory of a given canton (such as Nidwalden, for example).

Release

How could administrative detainees leave correctional labour facilities again? When was the main purpose of "(re-)education" pursued by administrative detention reached? Who decided when a detainee could be released? Was an administrative detainee really free when he/she left the correctional labour facility? The chapter on release practices is dedicated to these issues.

The release could be either final or on probation. For example, if in the Twenties a detainee wished to be released in advance from the "correctional labour facility for drunkards Fir Tree House" ("maison de travail pour buveurs, Sapinière"), which was part of the correctional labour facilities of Bellechasse in the Canton of Fribourg, at the end of the day the power to decide was in the hands of the facility director. His expert assessment was crucial for establishing the detainee's "recovery performance".

Img. 4.2 – Archives de l'Etat de Fribourg (AEF)/Staatsarchiv Freiburg (StAF), EB Div Photos 10 II, Bellechasse, « Photographie aérienne de Bellechasse », 1956-1972. [Photographe : B. Bachmann, Bern]

In this way, the inmates were put under strong pressure to comply and meet the expectations set in terms of work and behaviour during their detention. A release on probation, as transition between detention and freedom, essentially entailed - and not only in Bellechasse and in the Twenties - restrictions concerning the behaviour required during the probation period (for example, a ban on entering inns or abstinence from alcohol). Inmates released on probation were kept under surveillance by local authorities or employees of the supervision authority. If during the probation period they did not behave as prescribed, they risked to be locked up again in the correctional labour facility. The fact that many people were detained several times shows how difficult it was to escape the control mechanisms.

Epilogue: a quantitative approach towards a particular coercive measure with many shapes

Over 640 institutions have been chosen by a plethora of cantonal, municipal and private decision takers between 1930 and 1980 to confine individuals by administrative detention. The analyzed sources shed light on the different types of institutions with their spatial and chronological disposition. By outlining the relations between authorities and corresponding institutions in adjacent cantons, a network has emerged which dissolved cantonal and mere magisterial boundaries - the institutions of Bellechasse being one example of an intersection. These multilateral interactions on cantonal level started way before the intercantonal concordats have been established between the 1960ies and mid-seventies, which destined a few key institutions to hold inmates from all the participating cantons.

For the first time ever, institutional landmarks have been identified and characterized all over Switzerland and been put on interactive maps. The glossary-section documents the variety on cantonal laws and facilities thoroughly, but probably not exhaustively. A quantitative approach to the particular coercive measure leads to a profound knowledge of how this topography was built, has developed and functioned. These visualizations illustrate the working process from raw historical sources towards first research results and raise further questions that will be answered in the final publication of the Independent Expert Commission.

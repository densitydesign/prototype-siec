Introduction

Timeline

The timeline covers the period from 1930 to 1980. Our intent is to provide an overview of developments in the social history of Switzerland during that period. The timeline also charts the relevant legislative developments and, at the same time, indicates which detention facilities were opened or closed during those years. The simultaneous display of events at these three levels helps to situate the phenomenon of administrative detention in a larger historical context/scale.

  • Level 1, Social history: The introduction of social security measures in the 1940s (Old Age and Survivor Benefits Insurance, Disability Insurance, Family Allowances, Unemployment Insurance) – measures still in effect today – and Switzerland’s ratification of the European Convention on Human Rights in 1974, had an important influence on the decisions taken by the authorities, as they offered viable alternatives for dealing with poverty and so called social deviance.

  • Level 2, Legislation: This level shows the federal and cantonal laws that were of relevance to administrative detention measures. It makes clear the extent to which the use of administrative detention was influenced by cantonal structures. At the same time, a study of the wording of the laws reveals that the term “administrative detention” was only rarely employed; very often character descriptions are used for purposes of categorisation (e.g., indolent, dissolute, drunkard). This made it possible to take such social “abnormalities” and morally tinged categorisations as a basis for ordering administrative detention.

  • Level 3, Detention facilities: The majority of the detention facilities were established in the 19th and early 20th centuries. Between 1930 and 1980, they were supplemented by smaller facilities, which were used mainly for psychiatric cases. Beginning in the 1970s, there is a discernible trend towards the closing of district prisons, juvenile correction facilities, and labour colonies (presumably for purposes of centralisation under intercantonal conventions on correctional measures).

This segment is about the sources, their quality and validity. The information on which the charts are based was gathered from five sources respectively lists of the facilities and institutions that were used for correctional purposes and the execution of administrative detention orders. Those directories were compiles at different times and by different authors. Common to all of the directories is that they cover the whole of Switzerland. In addition, at the time when they were compiled, they all served as guides (reference works) for committing agencies and are comparable in the amount of detail they include.

They vary widely in the perspectives from which they were prepared, and thus focus on different types of institutions. Three of the directories contain all of the data we needed for preparing the charts (1954, 1965, 1980). For the other two, the lists of 1933 and the 1940ies, it was necessary for us to consult additional sources as well, in order to gather the complete information. Poorhouses are mentioned only in the first source, whereas the list of 1980 sheds light only on prisons:. This also illustrates one of the weaknesses of these sources, since e.g. poor houses played a decisive role in dealing with poverty among the elderly and for the internment of persons of advanced age.

In addition to the already mentioned mosaic-diagramm, the sankey shows the shifts of the observed institutions between the surveyed years. A total of 648 institutions are enlisted in the five directories, although not all of them necessarily appear in each individual survey (directory). How many of the institutions were open, but not surveyed by the authors and therefore are a blind spot in the data? The 1933 source is one of the most representative, as we find there all of the categories of facilities (poorhouses, juvenile reformatory, labour colonies, correctional labour facilities, institutions for persons with special needs, psychiatric facilities, alcoholic asylums) that are of interest to us in connection with the use of administrative detention. The category of “poorhouses” was left out in the other sources (and therefore labelled as uncertain); the focus of those sources is more on correctional labour and correctional facilities. In addition, some of the institutions were closed over the years, while new ones were opened.

2 - Geographic distribution of detention facilities

2.1 - Overview

This map provides a complete overview of the 648 facilities in operation in the years indicated. It is possible to identify areas of concentration and gaps in the geographic distribution. In 1954 for example, most of the surveyed facilities were spread all over the Mittelland with certain concentrations. As shown in the year of 1965 lots of intistutions are gathered in urban centers like Zurich, Basel, Berne and Geneva, but also in a rural areas like the Seeland.

2.2 - Distribution by facility type

The following types of detention facilities existed: forced labour institutions, labour colonies, juvenile reformatories, prisons, psychiatric institutions, municipal poorhouses (mentioned only in the 1933 source) and facilities for persons with special needs. All of these facility types were used for the confinement of administrative detainees. Conditions varied widely from canton to canton. With a closer look on the year 1965, prisons were quite evenly spread all over Switzerland, because most districts had their own small prison. Furthermore, educational institutions were located in urban areas. Many forced labour institutions happened to be in previously drained rural and plain areas such as the Seeland (Etablissements Bellechasse, Anstalten Witzwil, St. Johannsen, Arbeiterheim Tannenhof) or the Orbebene (Etablissements de la Plaine de l'Orbe).

2.3 - Institutional capacities

With the help of this map we would like to show how many places were available in each of the facilities. Here also, one can see how broad a range of facilities there was, from the smallest with a capacity of up to 19 places to the larger-sized ones with capacities of 150 or even 200 and more inmates.

In 1954, considering forced labor institutions, labor colonies and juvenile reformatories only, almost one fifth of the available space for inmates in Switzerland was situated in just five facilities of the Seeland area: Etablissements de Bellechasse, Institutions of Witzwil, Arbeiterheim Tannenhof, St. Johannsen and Staatliche Erziehungsheim für Knaben. This is 1520 places of an estimated total of 9000. Many inmates of these institutions were meant to work on the plains and contribute to the income of the institution. Today, Witzwil and Bellechasse are two of the largest producers of agricultural goods in Switzerland.

2.4 - Distribution by denomination

Religious confession and gender were two of the main criteria for deciding on the placement of detainees within the facilities: we distinguish between interdenominational, Protestand and Catholic ones. In the case of the poor houses the denomination remained often unknown. Interdenominational institutions were most likely state run and rather of large size, whereas Catholic and Protestant affiliated facilities were rather privately funded. For example, in 1965 a total of 84 percent of the institutions were interdenominational (the lion's share being district prisons), 11 percent Catholic and 5 percent Protestant. Facilities that were restricted to a single confession were in most cases educational ones. Amongst the Catholic ones are Schweizerisches Mädchenheim in Richterswil ZH, Kinderdörfli Rathausen LU and Institution Bon Pasteuer in Lully FR and amongst the Protestant ones are the Mütter- und Kinderheim in Lausen BL, Landheim Brüttisellen in Bassersdorf ZH and Gemeinnütziges Erziehungsheim Schloss Kasteln in Oberflachs AG.

2.5 - Distribution by gender

Gender was the second major criterion that was considered when choosing a facility . Sticking to the targeted year of 1965, the overall picture indicates, that 59 percent were foth both genders, 27 for male and 14 for female inmates only. A considerable amount of facilities with places for both men and women were district prisons. By leaving this type aside, the proportion would shift distinctively towards one half of the total for male inmates, and a quarter for female respectively for both gender each.

Internment and release

Placement in an institution

(Diagram field C) En Suisse, les procédures d’internement administratif reposaient sur un ensemble hétérogène et complexe de lois cantonales ainsi que sur le Code civil suisse. Chaque canton a élaboré ses voies de procédures et défini les autorités impliquées qu’elles soient communales, régionales ou cantonales. Il en résulte donc une grande diversité des formes que prend le processus menant à l’internement administratif. Dans le cas du Canton de Fribourg, les différentes mesures d’internements étaient essentiellement entre les mains du préfet, représentant du gouvernement dans son district et responsable du maintien de l’ordre public. Peu formalisée et souvent expéditive, ce type de procédure prévoyait que le préfet menait l’enquête, s’appuyant principalement sur les dires de la police et des autorités locales. Il prenait ensuite lui-même, seul, la décision d’internement. Un minimum de garanties juridiques était octroyé à la personne dénoncée par le droit d’être entendu et celui de recours auprès du gouvernement. Pour les Fribourgeois-es, l’internement avait généralement lieu à Bellechasse, établissement qui prenait aussi en charge des interné-e-s en provenance d’autres cantons suisses.

This example shows how the Bellechasse facilities served as the “final stop” in the administrative detention process. Bellechasse was used, as mentioned, not only for people from the canton of Fribourg, but also for detainees from other cantons. In other words, the cantonal governments concluded agreements with one another for the execution of administrative detention orders outside their own territory. Release

How could administrative detainees leave correctional labour facilities again? When was the main purpose of «(re-)education» pursued by administrative detention reached? Who decided when a detainee could be released? Was an administrative detainee really free when he/she left the correctional labour facility? The chapter on release practices is dedicated to these issues. The release could be either final or on probation. For example, if in the Twenties a detainee wished to be released in advance from the «correctional labour facility for drunkards Fir Tree House» («maison de travail pour buveurs, Sapinière»), which was part of the correctional labour facilities of Bellechasse in the Canton of Fribourg, at the end of the day the power to decide was in the hands of the facility director. His expert assessment was crucial for establishing the detainee’s «recovery performance». In this way, the inmates were put under strong pressure to comply and meet the expectations set in terms of work and behaviour during their detention. A release on probation, as transition between detention and freedom, essentially entailed – and not only in Bellechasse and in the Twenties – restrictions concerning the behaviour required during the probation period (for example, a ban on entering inns o abstinence from alcohol). Inmates released on probation were kept under surveillance by local authorities or employees of the supervision authority. If during the probation period they did not behave as prescribed, they risked to be locked up again in the correctional labour facility. The fact that many people were detained several times shows how difficult it was to escape the control mechanisms.
